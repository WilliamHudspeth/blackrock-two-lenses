# What Is BlackRock?
### The argument behind the valuation

Most valuations of BlackRock begin with an assumption so obvious it's never stated: *BlackRock is an asset manager.* The entire exercise then becomes a question of which asset-manager multiple to apply. This project starts somewhere else — with the claim that the assumption itself is the most important and least examined input in the whole model.

## Risk management was the founding act, not a feature

The standard story treats Aladdin — BlackRock's risk and portfolio platform — as a clever side business that emerged once the firm had scale. The history says the opposite.

BlackRock was founded in 1988 by Larry Fink and seven partners, seeded by Blackstone. The founding obsession was risk: two years earlier, Fink had lost a reported $100 million at First Boston on interest-rate risk his desk failed to measure, and the firm he built afterward was organized around never being blind to that risk again. The first employee hired, Charles Hallac, was put to work building the system that became Aladdin (Asset, Liability, Debt and Derivative Investment Network). It ran initially on a single workstation in a one-room office.

In other words, Aladdin did not appear *after* BlackRock got big. It grew up alongside the firm, as the institutionalized expression of the capability the firm was founded to have. BlackRock began licensing it to outside clients around 1999 and built BlackRock Solutions around it in 2000. The firm's own 2025 annual report describes Aladdin as having been "the backbone of our own growth."

The conclusion is not that *BlackRock succeeded because it has Aladdin* — that's the weak, promotional version, and it's too simple. The defensible version is this: **BlackRock's edge was never asset-gathering. It was a risk-management capability that preceded its scale and helped produce it. Aladdin is both a business and a window into that capability.**

## Aladdin as a window, not just a product

It's tempting to value Aladdin as software and stop there. But the more useful way to read it is as evidence. The platform exists because BlackRock institutionalized a way of thinking about portfolios — measuring what you own, not just trading it — and that habit shaped how the firm won mandates, retained clients, and earned the trust of the largest asset owners in the world. The technology is downstream of the culture. As one early architect of the platform put it, what matters is not the technology itself but how it is used.

This is why Aladdin's ~12% share of operating income understates its importance. It is a small revenue line attached to a large strategic fact: BlackRock sits inside the daily workflows of pensions, insurers, sovereign wealth funds, and other managers. That position is hard to win and harder to dislodge.

## The ecosystem: where the value may actually live

One thing that became clear in building this valuation is how difficult it is to keep BlackRock's businesses separate. The segments are not independent; they feed one another:

- iShares creates breadth of relationships across institutions and advisors.
- Those relationships lower the cost of Aladdin adoption.
- Aladdin deepens integration into client operations, raising switching costs.
- That integration creates a natural channel for infrastructure (GIP) and private-credit (HPS) mandates.
- Those private-market businesses reinforce BlackRock's relevance with the same clients — and generate more for Aladdin to measure.

The result is a firm whose value may come less from any single segment than from the way the segments reinforce one another. This is a stronger claim than "asset manager versus platform compounder." It is the claim that BlackRock may be worth more than the sum of its parts.

That phrase deserves suspicion, because it is what every conglomerate says. Markets usually apply a *discount*, not a premium, to "the parts reinforce each other." So the argument carries a burden of proof: it has to point to real reinforcement — rising retention, cross-sold mandates, a falling cost of winning the next dollar — rather than asserting synergy. Where the evidence is strongest is integration and switching cost; where it is weakest, today, is private credit.

## How this reconciles with the sum-of-the-parts model

A sum-of-the-parts valuation assumes the parts are separable and additive — which is, strictly, the opposite of the ecosystem claim. That tension is not a flaw in the model; it is the most important number in it.

Valued mechanically, segment by segment, BlackRock is worth roughly $846 a share before any cross-segment effect — close to the rate-sensitive read of ~$825. The model then adds a single line, a "platform / operating-leverage overlay" of about +$114 a share, to reach the platform-compounder value of ~$959. **That overlay is the ecosystem premium.** It is the dollar value of the claim that the whole exceeds the sum because the parts reinforce one another.

Stating it that way changes where the real debate sits. The argument over BlackRock is not whether iShares grows at 3% or 4%. It is whether that overlay should be +$114, zero, or negative. That single line is the most thesis-dependent and most attackable number in the valuation — and that is exactly where an honest disagreement about the company belongs.

## The flywheel runs in both directions

If the segments reinforce one another on the way up, they also transmit stress on the way down. The integration that compounds institutional trust in calm markets can propagate doubt in stressed ones. BlackRock's decision in early 2026 to gate roughly $1.2 billion of private-credit redemptions, with renewed pressure later in the year, is precisely this kind of event: a problem in one node that can erode confidence across the system the rest of the ecosystem depends on. A credible flywheel thesis prices the downside loop, not only the upside one. This is the main reason the current valuation — which appears to credit the upside loop in full — looks to be ahead of the evidence.

## The question that remains

So which is it: did Aladdin make BlackRock large, or did being large make Aladdin valuable? The honest answer is both, reflexively — and that reflexivity is the signature of a genuine compounder rather than a lucky one. The founding history matters here because it shows the capability came *first*. That is what keeps the argument from being circular, and what makes "BlackRock was never just an asset manager" a claim about the business rather than a slogan about the stock.
